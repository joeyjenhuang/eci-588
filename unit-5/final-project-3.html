Final Project: Data Sources & Student Examples

ECI 588: Text Mining in Education

Author

Dr. Shaun Kellogg

Published

April 13, 2025

Overview

As part of your final projects for ECI 586: Text Mining in Education, you’ll explore a research question of interest using text mining techniques applied to real-world datasets. This document showcases a selection of student data products ranging from interactive slide presentations and analytical reports to in-depth case studies highlighting how text data can reveal insights into learning, teaching, and academic communities. Additionally, I’ve curated list of public educational text datasets that you are more than welcome to use for your final project. These datasets span diverse educational contexts including K–12 classrooms, online tutoring sessions, and higher education settings, offering rich opportunities for text analysis, sentiment modeling, discourse research, and predictive modeling.

Student Data Products

Slides

  1. Academic Mental Health on Twitter
    By Jennifer Houchins
    This project explores public sentiment around academic mental health using Twitter data. Through sentiment analysis in R, the study investigates how users discuss mental health in academic contexts and identifies prevailing emotions and keywords across tweets.
    URL: https://jennhouchins.github.io/Sentiments/SentimentAnalysisinR.html#1

  2. North Carolina Goes Back to School
    By Tori Culler
    This analysis focuses on public discourse during the reopening of North Carolina schools in the context of COVID-19. Using text mining techniques, the project examines Twitter conversations to understand concerns, emotions, and topics prevalent in the back-to-school dialogue.
    URL: https://cullerth.github.io/wk_7_indpndnt_analysis/wk_7_presentation.html

Reports

  1. Comparing Duke, UNC, and NC State Twitter Activity: A Text Mining Approach
    By Soraya Campbell.
    This report compares the social media presence of three major North Carolina universities by analyzing tweets mentioning Duke, UNC, and NC State. It employs frequency analysis, sentiment classification, and topic modeling to uncover differences in public perception and communication patterns.
    URL: https://sorayaworldwide.github.io/DukeUNCNCStateTweets.html

Case Studies

  1. Predicting Non-success in a College Science Course
    By Rebecca Lucas
    This case study applies text mining and predictive modeling to student responses from a college science course. The goal is to identify linguistic patterns that may signal academic struggle or risk of non-completion, offering insights for early intervention.
    URL: https://rpubs.com/rmlucas/845863

  2. #BlackinSTEM and #DiversityinSTEM Twitter Analysis
    By Deijah Scales.
    This project analyzes Twitter discussions using the hashtags #BlackinSTEM and #DiversityinSTEM. It applies sentiment and topic analysis to highlight key themes, voices, and issues shared by users advocating for inclusion and equity in STEM fields.
    URL: https://rpubs.com/dsc21/845881

  3. Reddit Vaccine Myths Sentiment Analysis
    by Jessica Bethea
    This project analyzes sentiment within the r/VaccineMyths subreddit to understand how users discuss and perceive vaccines, particularly in the context of misinformation. By examining trends over time, the study explores whether user sentiment aligns with the subreddit’s goal of dispelling vaccine myths and identifies periods of heightened discussion.
    URL: https://rpubs.com/jmbethe2/983173

Educational Text Datasets

Below is a curated list of text-based public datasets that span K‑12 classrooms, higher education, online learning, and teacher development contexts. Each dataset includes a brief description, a source link, and the types of text data included. You are more than welcome to use any of the datasets listed below, or located a dataset of your own choosing.

TalkMoves Classroom Transcript Dataset (K‑12 Math)

  • Description: TalkMoves is a collection of 567 transcripts of K‑12 mathematics lessons, drawn from real classroom recordings​. Each transcript is segmented by speaker (teacher or student) and annotated at the sentence/utterance level for teacher and student “talk moves” (discursive moves based on accountable talk theory) as well as dialogue act labels​. This rich annotation highlights how teachers prompt discussion and how students respond, making it useful for discourse analysis or studying interaction patterns. The dataset is openly licensed (CC BY-NC-SA) and available for research use.

  • Source: The dataset is described in Suresh et al. Suresh et al. (2022) and is released via the authors’ repository​at. It can be accessed on GitHub at https://github.com/SumnerLab/TalkMoves.

  • Text Type: Full classroom discussion transcripts (teacher and student utterances), with discourse move annotations.

NCTE Elementary Classroom Transcripts (K‑12 Math)

  • Description: The NCTE dataset is the largest public collection of U.S. classroom transcriptsto date​. It contains 1,660 anonymized transcripts of 4th and 5th grade math lessons (45–60 minutes each) collected in the National Center for Teacher Effectiveness study. These transcripts include dialogue from whole-class and small-group instruction, and come with extensive metadata: turn-level codes for discourse moves, teacher effectiveness observation scores, teacher/student demographics, survey responses, and even student test score outcomes​. This makes it valuable for topic modeling of classroom talk, qualitative discourse analysis, or linking language patterns to educational outcomes. The data is fully anonymized and publicly shareable (with IRB clearance).

  • Source: The dataset is provided in an EdWorkingPaper by Demszky & Hill Demszky and Hill (2022) and is accessible via a GitHub repository at ​https://github.com/ddemszky/classroom-transcript-analysis.

  • Text Type: Elementary classroom transcripts (teacher and student dialogue in math lessons), plus associated survey and score data for context.

ASAP Automated Essay Scoring Dataset (K‑12 Student Writing)

  • Description: This dataset comes from the Hewlett Foundation’s Automated Student Assessment Prize. It includes eight sets of student essays written in response to different prompts, with each set containing several hundred to a few thousand essays​. The essays were written by students in grades 7–10 and range from roughly 150 to 550 words each​. All essays were hand-scored by human raters (with multiple scores per essay), providing ground truth labels for writing quality. Each essay set has unique characteristics (some prompts are narrative, others are persuasive or source-based), which is useful for topic modeling (to see themes students write about) or sentiment/affect analysis in student writing. The dataset is popularly used for building and evaluating essay scoring models.

  • Source: The ASAP dataset was originally released via a Kaggle competition (Hewlett Foundation, 2012) and is available for download​ at paperswithcode.com. Researchers often access it through the Kaggle platform at https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/asap-aes/data or here https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/lburleigh/asap-2-0.

  • Text Type: Student written essays responding to prompts, with human score annotations for each essay.

CIMA Tutoring Dialogues Dataset (Online Learning/Tutoring)

  • Description: CIMA is a large open-access dialogue dataset for tutoring, introduced by Stasaski et al. Stasaski, Kao, and Hearst (2020). It contains one-on-one tutoring conversations that were generated via a novel crowdsourcing method: crowdworkers role-played as students and tutors, producing extended tutoring dialogues​. The student role-players work through educational exercises and the tutor role-players respond with various instructional strategies (asking questions, giving hints, providing feedback, etc.). Notably, multiple alternative tutor responses were collected for the same student query, and students were exposed to overlapping concepts across exercises​. This dataset reflects some pedagogical dialogue norms (e.g. when to hint vs. when to question) and is ideal for conversation analysis, tutoring strategy mining, or training dialogue systems for education.

  • Source: The CIMA dataset is publicly available under a Creative Commons license​ and accompanies the ACL BEA 2020 paper by Stasaski et al. Stasaski, Kao, and Hearst (2020), and the data (with annotations of tutor/student actions) can be obtained from the authors’ GitHub repository at https://github.com/kstats/CIMA.

  • Text Type: Simulated tutoring conversation logs (dialogue between a tutor and a student in a text-based tutoring session), including annotations of tutor and student action types.

Teacher-Student Chatroom Corpus (TSCC) (Online ESL Tutoring)

  • Description: The TSCC is a corpus of online English-as-a-second-language tutoring sessions conducted via text chat. The first version (Caines et al. 2020) contained 102 chat dialogues between two human tutors and eight ESL students​. These dialogues comprise about 13.5k conversational turns and 133k words in total​. The content is authentic (students and professional teachers in a live chatroom) and has been anonymized and annotated for research. An updated version TSCC v2 has expanded to 260 total lessons with annotations for dialogue acts, pedagogical strategies, and teacher self-evaluation codes​​. This corpus is well-suited for discourse analysis (especially tutor feedback, error correction, Q&A patterns) and for training or evaluating models on dialogue in an educational context (e.g. predicting student language proficiency or tutor responses).

  • Source: The TSCC is freely available for research use​. Researchers can request the data (the 2020 release and the 2022 expanded release) from the authors a by visiting forms.gle/pKc48WMhnySC8zDk9 to review the license and submit a data request. No special license fees or logins are required beyond agreeing to research use terms.

  • Text Type: Text-based tutoring session transcripts (teacher–student chat logs for English language lessons), with annotations for dialogue moves and error corrections.

RateMyProfessors.com Reviews (Higher Ed Student Feedback)

  • Description: This is a large-scale dataset of student reviews of college instructors, scraped from RateMyProfessors.com. It includes data for nearly 1 million professors across many universities​ with each professor entry containing several attributes (department, school, overall rating scores, etc.) and open-ended student comments. The key text data here are the comments that students wrote about their professors’ teaching​. These comments often express student opinions, praise, criticisms, and suggestions, making the data rich for sentiment analysis (e.g., identifying positive vs. negative sentiment about teaching) and topic modeling (e.g., common themes in student praise or complaints). The dataset also includes numeric ratings (quality, difficulty, etc.), which can be used alongside the text for predictive modeling.

  • Source: A compiled version of RateMyProfessors data (millions of reviews) has been published by researchers and is available on data repositories. For example, He et al. (2020) released a 5GB dataset on Mendeley Data​ at which includes a combined CSV of professor info and reviews. Smaller samples and derived datasets are also on Kaggle and other platforms.

  • Text Type: Student evaluation comments about instructors (short reviews in plain text), accompanied by structured fields like numerical ratings and tags.

Educator Online Q&A Forums (Teacher Development)

  • Description: Teachers and educators often exchange knowledge in online forums. One example is the Stack Exchange network’s sites for educators, such as Mathematics Educators or Computer Science Educators. These are question-and-answer forums for teachers to discuss pedagogy, curriculum, classroom management, and professional challenges. For instance, the Computer Science Educators Stack Exchange is described as “a question and answer site for those involved in the field of teaching Computer Science”​. The datasets from these sites consist of community-generated questions, answers, and discussions. They are useful for text analyses like topic modeling (to find common discussion topics in teacher communities) or sentiment/opinion mining (e.g., teachers’ attitudes toward certain educational technologies or policies). They can also support discourse analysis of how educators seek and share knowledge online.

  • Source: Stack Exchange content is publicly licensed (Creative Commons) and available via data dumps or the Stack Exchange Data Explorer. Instructions for downloading the Stack Exchange data dump for the educator sites can be found here: https://matheducators.stackexchange.com/help/data-dumps.

  • Text Type: Question posts and answer posts written by educators (with comment threads in some cases), i.e. online discussion content among teachers focused on teaching practice.
